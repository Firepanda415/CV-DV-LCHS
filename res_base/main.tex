\title{Quantum Differential Equation Solver via Hybrid Oscillator-Qubit Linear Combination of Hamiltonian Simulations}
\author{Elin Ranjan Das, Muqing Zheng, Rishab Dutta, Ang Li, Timothy Stavenger, Yuan Liu}
\date{August 2025}


\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
    This paper introduces an advanced quantum simulation framework for solving linear ordinary and partial differential equations (ODEs/PDEs) using a hybrid oscillator-qubit architecture. By joining the Linear Combination of Hamiltonian Simulation (LCHS) paradigm with continuous-variable (CV) auxiliary registers, the proposed method replaces the discrete quadrature registers used in qubit-only (discrete-variable) LCHS. This hybrid approach leverages the infinite-dimensional Hilbert space of harmonic oscillators to eliminate the discretization step, thereby removing the associated truncation errors inherent in discrete LCHS integrals. This eliminates the $O(\log M)$ ancillary qubit requirement and significantly reduces circuit depth; for a 100-step heat equation benchmark, the hybrid method requires 400 CNOTs compared to nearly 4,800 in a compressed discrete-variable implementation. We provide a comprehensive methodology for state preparation and hybrid evolution, establishing rigorous theoretical error bounds for Fock space truncation and product-formula approximations. The framework's utility is demonstrated through circuit-level realizations for representative physical systems, including a damped harmonic oscillator and the $d$-dimensional heat equation under various boundary conditions. Numerical experiments confirm that the hybrid CV-DV architecture achieves high fidelity (above 99.89\%) with significantly more compact oracle representations compared to discrete baselines. Our findings identify oscillator-state synthesis as the primary performance bottleneck and position hybrid LCHS as a resource-efficient pathway for high-precision quantum differential equation solving.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The ability of controllable quantum systems to efficiently emulate complex quantum dynamics has motivated substantial theoretical and experimental efforts. Accurate simulation of quantum many-body systems underpins advances in condensed matter physics, quantum chemistry, high-energy physics, and materials science~\cite{Clinton_2024}. However, realizing scalable and precise quantum simulation remains a central challenge due to hardware limitations and algorithmic constraints.

One of the most powerful algorithmic paradigms for digital quantum simulation is Hamiltonian simulation via linear combinations of unitaries (LCU)~\cite{2012}. In this framework, the target Hamiltonian is decomposed into a weighted sum of efficiently implementable unitary operators, enabling high-precision time evolution with provable complexity bounds. Building on this idea, linear combination of Hamiltonian simulation (LCHS)~\cite{LCHS1,LCHS2} methods provide a systematic way to construct evolution operators while maintaining controllable error scaling. However, existing implementations of LCHS rely exclusively on discrete-variable (qubit-based) architectures, where continuous degrees of freedom must first be discretized. This discretization introduces truncation errors and often requires large qubit overhead to achieve high fidelity, particularly when simulating systems with inherently continuous variables.

Continuous-variable (CV) quantum systems such as harmonic oscillators realized in photonic modes or superconducting cavities naturally encode infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces. These systems are well suited for representing bosonic modes and field-theoretic models without the need for coarse discretization. Meanwhile, qubit-based platforms provide strong nonlinearity, robust control, and efficient implementation of conditional operations. Hybrid oscillator-qubit architectures combine the advantages of both paradigms, offering expanded computational expressiveness and improved resource efficiency.

In this work, we propose an advanced method for quantum simulation based on linear combination of Hamiltonian simulation within a hybrid oscillator-qubit architecture. By leveraging continuous-variable encodings for naturally bosonic degrees of freedom and discrete-variable control for selective unitary operations, our approach eliminates the discretization step that typically introduces truncation errors in purely qubit-based LCHS implementations. The resulting framework enables more faithful representation of continuous dynamics while preserving the algorithmic strengths of linear-combination-based simulation techniques.

\section{Motivation}







LCHS provides an optimal state preparation cost and a near-optimal scaling in matrix queries on all parameters for solving linear ordinary differential equations (ODEs) in the form of
\begin{align}
    \frac{\dd u(t)}{\dd t} = -A(t)u(t) + b(t), \quad u(0) = u_0  \label{eq:lchs-ODE}
\end{align}
where $t \in [0, T]$, $A(t) \in \Cbb^{N \times N}$ and $u(t), b(t) \in \Cbb^{N}$. The solution of the ODE~\eqref{eq:lchs-ODE} has the explicit form
\begin{align}
    u(T) &= \Tcal e^{- \int^T_0 A(s) \dd s}u_0 + \int^T_0 \Tcal e^{-\int^T_s A(s')\dd s'}b(s) \dd s \label{eq:exp-sol}
\end{align}
where $\Tcal$ is the time-ordering operator. To start with, the coefficient matrix $A(t)$ is first decomposed into \[A(t) = L(t) + iH(t)\] via Cartesian decomposition \[L(t) = \frac{A(t) + A^\dagger(t)}{2}, H(t) = \frac{A(t) - A^\dagger(t)}{2i}.\] It is assumed that $L(t) \succeq 0$. Otherwise, we can shift the eigenspectrum of $L(t)$ by substituting $u(t) = e^{ct}v(t)$ and use $v(t)$ for the ODE system so that $L(t) + cI \succeq 0$, where $-c$ is the minimum of the smallest eigenvalues of $L(t)$. LCHS provides that 
\begin{align}
    \Tcal e^{- \int^t_0 A(s) \dd s} &= \int_{\Rbb}  g(k) \left[\Tcal e^{-i \int^t_0 (kL(s)+H(s))\dd s} \right] \dd k \label{eq:LCHS-sol-homo} \\
    \int^t_0 \Tcal e^{-\int^t_s A(s')\dd s'}b(s) \dd s &= \int^t_0 \int_\Rbb g(k) \left[ \Tcal e^{-i \int^t_s (kL(s')+H(s'))\dd s'} \right]  b(s) \dd k \dd s   \label{eq:LCHS-sol-inhomo}
\end{align}
where $g(k):=\frac{f(k)}{1-ik}$ for some kernel function $f(k)$ and $t \in [0,T]$. The choice of $f(k)$ that leads to near-optimal complexity dependency on all parameters is $f(k) = \frac{e^{2^{\beta}}}{2\pi e^{(1+ik)^\beta}}$, where $\beta \in (0,1)$ is a user-defined constant~\cite{LCHS2}.
Note that the terms in brackets in Eq.~\eqref{eq:LCHS-sol-homo} and~\eqref{eq:LCHS-sol-inhomo} are unitary. Thus, LCHS further truncates and discretizes the integrals in Eq.~\eqref{eq:LCHS-sol-homo} and~\eqref{eq:LCHS-sol-inhomo} into a sum of unitaries, employing state preparation circuits for $\ket{u_0}:=\frac{u_0}{\|u_0\|}$ and $\ket{b(s)}:=\frac{b(s)}{\|b(s)\|}$ and the linear combination of unitaries (LCU) circuits to prepare the solution state $\ket{u(T)}:=\frac{u(T)}{\|u(T)\|}$~\cite{LCHS1,LCHS2}.




Combining with the finite difference method and linearization methods, for example, Carleman linearization~\cite{liu2021carleman}, various non-linear partial differential equations can also be converted into a linear ODE system that is solvable by LCHS. However, the number of terms in the discretized LCHS integral, denoted by $M$, brings a huge cost on quantum resources in the circuit implementation level for either near-term or early fault-tolerant quantum machines. For the complementary solution, $M$ has complexity
\begin{align*}
        M \in \Ocal\left( T \max_t \|L(t)\|\left(\log \frac{1}{\epsilon}\right)^{1+1/\beta} \right)
\end{align*}
where $\epsilon$ is the total error incurred by LCHS~\cite{LCHS2}. The corresponding LCU circuit requires $\lceil\log_2(M)\rceil$ ancillary qubits, and more problematically, $\lceil\log_2(M)\rceil$-controlled multiple-qubit unitary operators for Hamiltonian simulations. In this case, for a time-independent ODE, the value of $M$ can be in the $10^6$ magnitude when $\epsilon$ is around $10^{-5}$, $\beta=0.8$, $T=1000$, and $\|L\|=1$. More exact scalings of LCHS are provided in~\cite{pocrnic2025constant}. 




Inspired by the continuous LCU in~\cite{bell2025co}, a hybrid CV-DV LCHS is a promising direction to bring down the resource cost and, ideally, remove truncation and discretization errors in the integrals calculation. Specifically, continuous LCU can use $\Ocal(1)$ number of ancillary oscillators, along with CV state preparation and CV measurements, replace $\lceil\log_2(M)\rceil$ ancillary qubits in the DV LCU circuit, while the unitaries for Hamiltonian evolution remain in qubit space. Continuous LCU circuit can compute the integrals in Eq.~\eqref{eq:LCHS-sol-homo} and~\eqref{eq:LCHS-sol-inhomo} without truncation and discretization on integrals, whereas truncations errors may still persist in CV state preparation and hybrid Hamiltonian simulation.

\section{Methodology}





Let us consider a time-independent homogeneous ODE system, where $A(t):=A$ and $b(t) := 0$ for all $t$. From Eq.~\eqref{eq:exp-sol} and~\eqref{eq:LCHS-sol-homo}, the complementary solution becomes
\begin{align}
    u(t) &= \left[\int_{\Rbb}  g(k)  e^{-i t (kL+H)}  \dd k \right] u_0 \label{eq:sol-homo-lchs}
\end{align}
for $t \in [0,T]$. Using a single oscillator for the ancillary in continuous LCU, the qubits that synthesize unitary $e^{-i t (kL+H)}$ need to couple with this ancillary oscillator, leading to the joint evolution
\(
    e^{-it ( L \otimes \hat{k} + H \otimes I)},
\)
where $\hat{k}$ is one of the canonical conjugation operators ($\hat x$ and $\hat p$) and $I$ is the identity operator. That is to say, the qumode remains untouched when $H$ acts on the qubit system. 

Before forming the circuit for the joint evolution, an appropriate function $g(k)$ has to be obtained. To do this, we need to prepare and post-select two appropriate CV states. 

Let \(\ket{\Psi}=\ket{\psi}_{\rm osc}\ket{u_0}_q\) be the preparation state, where $\ket{\psi}_{\rm osc}$ is the initial oscillator state and $\ket{u_0}$ is the initial qubit state. 
Let \(\bra{\Phi}=\bra{\phi}_{\rm osc}\otimes \mathbf{I}_q\) be the post-selection projector, where $\bra{\phi}_{\rm osc}$ acts only on the oscillator and the qubit remains untouched. 

If we choose the position operator $\hat x$ for $k$, then according to ref.~\cite{bell2025co}, the qumode sandwich gives
\begin{align}
     \bra{\Phi}  e^{-it ( \hat{x} \otimes  L + \mathbf{I}_{\rm osc} \otimes  H)} \ket{\Psi} = \bigg[\int_{\mathbb{R}} \dd x \, \phi^*(x) \psi(x)    e^{-it ( xL + H)}\bigg]\ket{u_0}_q.
\end{align}
Comparing to the Eq.~\eqref{eq:sol-homo-lchs}, this gives \(g(x)= \phi^*(x) \psi(x)\). Namely, we need to engineer the preparation and post-selection states to obtain an appropriate $g(x)$ that satisfies LCHS's requirements. 

We choose the post-selection state $\ket{\phi}$ to be a squeezed vacuum, whose position-space wavefunction is 
\begin{align}
    \phi_r(x):=S(r,0)\phi_0(x)=\bigg(\frac{2}{e^{2r}\pi}\bigg)^{1/4} e^{-x^2/e^{2r}}=\bigg(\frac{2}{\sigma^2\pi}\bigg)^{1/4} e^{-x^2/\sigma^2}
\end{align} 
with squeezing parameter $r$ and wavefunction standard deviation $\sigma=e^r$, and this gives the corresponding initial state to be prepared 
\begin{align}
    \psi_r(x):=\bigg(\frac{\sigma^2\pi}{2}\bigg)^{1/4}g(x)e^{x^2/\sigma^2}= \mathcal{N}g(x)e^{x^2/\sigma^2},
\end{align}
where both $r =0$ and $r \rightarrow \infty$ cases will be discussed in Section \ref{ss:state-prep}.

This leads to our formulation of a CV/DV LCHS proposition:
\begin{proposition}[Continuous-discrete variable LCHS]
Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ be a matrix decomposed into its Hermitian and anti-Hermitian parts such that $A = L + iH$, where:
    $$L = \frac{A + A^{\dagger}}{2} \succeq 0, \quad H = \frac{A - A^{\dagger}}{2i}$$
    Then the state $\ket{u(t)} = e^{-At}\ket{u(0)}$ can be represented using continuous-variable (CV) auxiliary registers. 
    The evolution is embedded into a higher-dimensional unitary operator acting on the combined system of an ancillary oscillators and the primary quantum register:
    \begin{align}
        \ket{u(t)}
        &= (\bra{\phi}_{\rm osc}\otimes\mathbf{I}_q)  e^{-it ( \hat{x} \otimes  L+ \mathbf{I}_{\rm osc} \otimes  H )} (\ket{\psi}_{\rm osc}\otimes\ket{u_0}_q)
    \end{align}


    \textit{where $\ket{\psi}_{\rm osc}$ and $\ket{\phi}_{\rm osc}$ are squeezed CV states such that $\phi^*(x)\psi(x)=g(x)$.}
\end{proposition}

\subsection{State Preparation}


\label{ss:state-prep}




We now consider two special cases of $\psi_r(x)$ preparation and their implications.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{r=0~(\sigma=1):}$ This leads to the post-selection state being the vacuum state $\phi_0(x)$. However, our preparation state becomes
    \begin{align}
        \psi_0(x) := \mathcal{N}g(x)e^{x^2}
    \end{align}
    which is not a physically realizable state, as $\psi_0(x)\notin L^2(\mathbb{R}).$ 
    
    This gives us an additional condition imposed on the $g(x)$ for CV-DV LCHS: 
    \begin{align}
        \exists\ r>0\ \text{s.t}\ \int_\mathbb{R} \dd x |g(x)|^2 e^{\frac{2x^2}{\sigma^2}} <\infty
    \end{align}

    \item $\mathbf{r\to\infty~ (\sigma\to\infty):}$ This leads to $\phi_r(x)$ being the infinitely squeezed vacuum state. Then our preparation state is simply the kernel itself:
    \begin{align}
        \psi_\infty(x) := \lim_{r\to\infty} \psi_r(x)= \mathcal{N}g(x)
    \end{align}
\end{itemize}
The initial state $\ket{\psi_r}$ can be approximated via a finite squeezed Fock expansion
\begin{align}
    \psi_r(x)&=\mathcal{N}g(x)e^{x^2/\sigma^2} \approx \sum_{{\mathfrak n} = 0}^{N} C_{\mathfrak n} \braket{x|S(r,0)|{\mathfrak n}}=\psi'(x)
\end{align}
where coefficients $\{C_{\mathfrak n}\}$ are overlaps
\begin{align}
    C_{\mathfrak n} &= \braket{{\mathfrak n}|S^\dagger(r',0)|\psi_r} = \int_{\mathbb{R}} \dd x \, \phi^*_{\mathfrak n}(x/\sigma')\,e^{-x^2/\sigma^2} \psi_r(x)
\end{align}
This expression simplifies to
\begin{align}
    C_{\mathfrak n}= \sqrt{\frac{\sigma}{\sigma'}}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^{\mathfrak n}{\mathfrak n}!}}\int_{\mathbb{R}}\dd x\,  H_{\mathfrak n}(x/\sigma')g(x)e^{-\gamma x^2}
    &= \int_{\mathbb{R}}\frac{\dd x\, e^{2^\beta}H_{\mathfrak n}(x/\sigma')e^{-\gamma x^2}}{2\pi e^{{(1+ix)}^\beta}(1-ix)}
    \label{eq:coefficients}
\end{align}
where \(\gamma=\frac{1}{\sigma'^2}-\frac{1}{\sigma^2}=\frac{1}{e^{2r'}}-\frac{1}{e^{2r}},\quad r'<r\). While eq.~\eqref{eq:coefficients} can have analytical forms depending on specific values of $\beta,$ it is not straightforward to derive a generalized expression. Two examples of closed analytic form for the limit values of $\beta=\{0,1\}$ are given in Appendix~\ref{ss:analytic}.

\subsection{Error bound estimation for state preparation}

Ideally we want to realize $\psi_r(x)=g(x)e^{x^2{e^{-2r}}}$ as close as possible using Gaussian (Fock) states. Previously, authors from \cite{Jin_2024} showed that sub-optimal kernel function $f(k)=\frac{1}{\pi(1+ik)}$ could be realized with a squeezed vacuum state with maximum fidelity of 98.6\% with a squeezing parameter of 0.925. However, we show that the near-optimal kernel function can be realized instead with a superposition of squeezed Fock states for arbitrary closeness, where the approximation error is bounded by the Fock cutoff level (or the oscillator energy) and the squeezing parameter:

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm: state-prep}
Let $\psi_r(x)=\mathcal{N}g(x)e^{x^2{e^{-2r}}}$ denote the ideal prepared state, where $g(x)$ is analytic in an infinite strip \(\mathcal{S}_{\rho} := \{ z \in \mathbb{C} : \Im(z) \in [-\rho, \rho] \}\) for some \(\rho=1-\epsilon\) with \(\epsilon\to0\). Let $\psi_{N,r'}$ be the truncated approximation in the squeezed Fock subspace $\mathbb H_N := \mathrm{span}\{\psi_{\mathfrak n}(x/\sigma')\}_{{\mathfrak n}=0}^N$, where $\sigma':=e^{r'}$ acts as a scaling factor. We define the orthogonal projection operator $\Pi^{F,r'}_N:L^2(\mathbb{R})\rightarrow \mathbb{H}_N$ such that for any $f\in L^2({\mathbb{R}})$: 
$$\left(\Pi_N^{F,r'} f\right)(x) = \sum_{\mathfrak{n}=0}^N C_{\mathfrak{n}} \psi_{\mathfrak{n}}(x/\sigma')$$
where the coefficients $C_{\mathfrak{n}}$ are the overlaps $\langle f, \psi_{\mathfrak{n}} \rangle$.
Then, for any target accuracy $\epsilon_s>0$,
there exists a Fock cutoff $N\in\mathbb{N}$ and a squeezing parameter $r>0$
such that
\[
\|\psi_{N,r'}-\psi_r\|_2 < \epsilon_s .
\]
In particular, it is sufficient to choose $N$ and $r$ in
\[
N = \mathcal O\!\left(\frac{1}{e^{-2r'}}\log^2 \frac{1}{\epsilon_s}\right),
\qquad
r = \mathcal O\!\left(\log \frac{1}{\epsilon_s}\right).
\]
\end{theorem}

Define the truncated approximation as the projection of \(g\),

    $$\lVert \psi_{N,r} -\psi_r\rVert\leq \lVert g - \Pi_N^F g\rVert + \lVert g(1- e^{k^2 e^{-2r}})\rVert$$
    
    According to~\cite[Thm.~3.8 \& Sec.~5.2]{wang2025convergenceanalysishermiteapproximations}, the projection error onto a basis with scaling factor $\lambda=1/\sigma'=e^{-r'}$ is governed by the analyticity strip $\rho$. The error bound is given by

    \lVert g - \Pi^{F,r'}_N g \rVert_2 \leq \mathcal{K} N^{1/4} e^{-\lambda\rho \sqrt{2N}}.

    The first inequality implies 
        $$r \geq (1/2)\log(2\mathcal M/\epsilon_s),$$

For the second inequality, while the $N^{1/4}$ prefactor suggests a technical scaling involving the Lamber W function, in the asymptotic limit $N\rightarrow\infty,$ the root-exponential term dominates, and we ignore the prefactor.  Hence, this yields, $$\sqrt{2N} \geq \frac{1}{\lambda\rho} \log\left(\frac{2\mathcal{K}}{\epsilon_s}\right),$$ which implies $N \geq (1/(2\rho^2e^{-2r'})) \log^2(2\mathcal{K}/\epsilon_s)$, that is,
     $N =\mathcal O\left(\frac{1}{e^{-2r'}}\log^2\frac{1}{\epsilon_s}\right)$. This establishes the claimed resource scaling.

\subsection{Trotter-Suzuki approximation for Hamiltonian evolution}
\label{sec:trotter}
Since the hybrid Hamiltonian $\hat{x}\otimes  L+I\otimes  H$ is not directly realizable as a native interaction, its time evolution is synthesized via a Trotter-Suzuki decomposition into a sequence of implementable operations. 
Let $\delta t = t/n$, where $t$ is the total simulation time and $n$ is the number of Trotter steps. Throughout this subsection we write $\hat x$ for the oscillator position operator, and the norms $\|\hat x\|$ below are understood on a suitably truncated or energy-constrained CV subspace.
\[
    U(t)=e^{-it(\hat x\otimes L+I\otimes H)}
    \approx
    \left(e^{-i\delta t(\hat x\otimes L)}e^{-i\delta t(I\otimes H)}\right)^n.
\]
For a single Trotter step, the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff expansion gives
\[
e^{-i\delta t(\hat x\otimes L)}e^{-i\delta t(I\otimes H)}
=
\exp\!\left(
-i\delta t(\hat x\otimes L+I\otimes H)
-\frac{(\delta t)^2}{2}[\hat x\otimes L,\,I\otimes H]
+\mathcal O((\delta t)^3)
\right).
\]
Accordingly, the outer split contributes
\[
\mathcal O\!\left(\frac{t^2}{n}\,\|[\hat x\otimes L,\,I\otimes H]\|\right)
=
\mathcal O\!\left(\frac{t^2}{n}\,\|\hat x\|\,\|[L,H]\|\right)
\]
to the first-order error. If only this outer split were present, achieving tolerance $\epsilon_t$ would require
\begin{equation}
    n = \mathcal{O}\!\left(
    \frac{t^2}{\epsilon_t}\,
    \|\hat{x}\|\,\|[L,H]\|
    \right).
\end{equation}

Suppose that the Hamiltonians admit Pauli decompositions of the form
\begin{equation}
     L = \sum_{i=1}^{N_L} \alpha_i P_i,
    \qquad
     H = \sum_{j=1}^{N_H} \beta_j Q_j,
\end{equation}
where $P_i$ and $Q_j$ are Pauli strings. Each exponential can then be further approximated using a first-order product formula as
\begin{align}
    e^{-i\delta t(\hat x \otimes L)} \approx
    \prod_{i=1}^{N_L} e^{-i\delta t \alpha_i (\hat x \otimes P_i)},\quad
    e^{-i\delta t(I \otimes H)} \approx
    \prod_{j=1}^{N_H} e^{-i\delta t \beta_j (I \otimes Q_j)}.
\end{align}
Combining these decompositions yields
\begin{equation}
    U(t) \approx
    \left(
    \prod_{i=1}^{N_L} e^{-i\delta t \alpha_i (\hat x \otimes P_i)}
    \prod_{j=1}^{N_H} e^{-i\delta t \beta_j (I \otimes Q_j)}
    \right)^n,
\end{equation}
Accumulating the outer split and the intra-block Pauli splittings over $n$ steps gives the schematic first-order bound
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_{\mathrm{tot}} = \underbrace{\mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{t^2}{n}\|\hat{x}\|\,\|[L,H]\|\right)}_{\text{outer split}} + \underbrace{\mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{t^2}{n}\|\hat{x}\|^2\sum_{i<k}|\alpha_i\alpha_k|\,\|[P_i,P_k]\|\right)}_{\text{inner split of }L} + \underbrace{\mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{t^2}{n}\sum_{j<\ell}|\beta_j\beta_\ell|\,\|[Q_j,Q_\ell]\|\right)}_{\text{inner split of }H}.
\label{eq:two-layer-error}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Higher-order product-formula simulation.}
For an even order $p=2k$, the $p$-th order Suzuki formula yields
\begin{equation}
    U(t)
    =
    e^{-it(\hat{x}\otimes L+I\otimes H)}
    \approx
    \left(S_p(\delta t)\right)^n,
\end{equation}
with error scaling
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon_{\mathrm{tot}}
    =
    \mathcal{O}\!\left(
    \frac{t^{p+1}}{n^p}\,
    \|\hat x\|\,
    \Gamma_p( L, H)
    \right),
\end{equation}
For operators $X$ and $Y$, we define the $r$-fold nested commutator recursively by
\begin{align}
    [X^{(0)},Y] := Y, \quad
    [X^{(r)},Y] := [X,[X^{(r-1)},Y]].
\end{align}
Then we can define $\Gamma_p( L, H)$ in terms of nested commutators as
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_p( L, H)
    =
    \max_{\substack{r+s=p\\ r,s\ge 0}}
    \left\{
    \bigl\|[ L^{(r)},[ H^{(s)}, L]]\bigr\|,
    \;
    \bigl\|[ L^{(r)},[ H^{(s)}, H]]\bigr\|
    \right\}.
\end{equation}
For practical purposes, this quantity admits the upper bound
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_p( L, H)
    \le
    C_p(\| L\|+\| H\|)^{p-1}\|[ L, H]\|.
\end{equation}
Consequently,
\begin{equation}
    n = \mathcal{O}\!\left(t^{1+1/p} \left(\frac{\|\hat x\|\Gamma_p( L, H)}{\epsilon_t}\right)^{1/p}\right).
\end{equation}
Moreover, since $[\hat{x}\otimes L, I\otimes H] = \hat{x}\otimes[ L, H]$, we have the bound
\begin{equation}
    \|[\hat{x}\otimes L, I\otimes H]\|
    \le
    \|\hat{x}\|\,\|[ L, H]\|,
\end{equation}
where $\|\hat x\|$ is evaluated on the same truncated or energy-constrained CV subspace as above.







\paragraph{Gate counts.}
Let $m_p$ denote the number of exponentials appearing in a single application of the $p$-th order Suzuki formula $S_p(\delta t)$.
For the standard recursive construction, one has
\begin{equation}
    m_{2}=3,
    \qquad
    m_{2k}=5\,m_{2k-2},
    \qquad
    \Rightarrow\qquad
    m_{p}=3\cdot 5^{(p/2)-1}.
\end{equation}

The total number of CNOT gates scales as
\begin{equation}
    N_{\mathrm{CNOT}}
    =
    \!\left(
    2nm_{p}\,
    \sum_{k=1}^{N_L+N_H} (w(P_k)-1)
    \right),
    \label{eq:cnot_scaling_suzuki}
\end{equation}
where $\{P_k\}$ denotes the collection of Pauli strings arising from the Pauli decompositions of $ L$ and $ H$.

Moreover, each exponential of the form $e^{-i\theta(\hat{x}\otimes P_i)}$ contributes a hybrid CV-DV interaction. Let $c_p$ denote the number of such hybrid exponentials within a single application of $S_p(\delta t)$. For the standard Suzuki construction, one has $c_p=\Theta(m_p)$, and therefore the total number of hybrid gates scales as $N_{\mathrm{hyb}} = O\!\left(nc_{p}N_L\right).$



At this point, the missing piece for the whole hybrid CV-DV LCHS circuit is the oracle syntheses for matrices $L$ and $H$, whose basis-gate implementations are highly dependent on the structures of both matrices and are very problem-specific. Many previous works in the DV regime tackle this problem by decomposing coefficient matrices into linear combinations of Pauli matrices, for example, hyperbolic partial differential equations~\cite{PhysRevResearch.6.033246} and advection-diffusion equation~\cite{alipanah2025quantum}. Borrowing their designs and following the circuit synthesis in~\cite{bell2025co}, the evolution of decomposed Hamiltonians coupled with oscillators can be constructed in CV-DV circuits.




\subsection{Inhomogeneous Time-Independent Solution}

Let us next consider the time-independent inhomogeneous ODE, where $A(t):=A=L+iH$ and $b(t):=b$ for all $t$. Starting from Eq.~\eqref{eq:LCHS-sol-inhomo} and introducing the elapsed time $\tau:=t-s$, the inhomogeneous contribution becomes
\begin{align}
    u_{\mathrm{inh}}(t)
    := \int_0^t e^{-A(t-s)} b \, \dd s
    = \int_0^t e^{-A\tau} b \, \dd \tau
    = \int_0^t \int_\Rbb g(k) e^{-i \tau (kL+H)} b \, \dd k \, \dd \tau .
    \label{eq:LCHS-inhomo-cv}
\end{align}
To embed Eq.~\eqref{eq:LCHS-inhomo-cv} into a hybrid oscillator-qubit system, let
\[
    \mathcal{H}_K = L^2(\Rbb), \qquad
    \mathcal{H}_{\tau} = L^2(\Rbb), \qquad
    \mathcal{H}_q = \Cbb^N,
\]
where the first oscillator encodes the LCHS variable $k$, the second oscillator encodes the elapsed time $\tau$, and the qubit register stores the source vector $b$. In the generalized eigenbases $\{\ket{k}\}_{k \in \Rbb}$ and $\{\ket{\tau}\}_{\tau \in \Rbb}$, define
\begin{align}
    \hat{k} = \int_{\Rbb} k \ketbra{k}{k} \, \dd k, \qquad
    \hat{\tau} = \int_{\Rbb} \tau \ketbra{\tau}{\tau} \, \dd \tau .
\end{align}
Choose oscillator preparation and post-selection states
\begin{align}
    \ket{\psi_K} &= \int_{\Rbb} \psi_K(k)\ket{k}\,\dd k, \qquad
    \bra{\phi_K} = \int_{\Rbb} \phi_K^*(k)\bra{k}\,\dd k, \\
    \ket{\psi_\tau^{(t)}} &= \int_{\Rbb} \psi_\tau^{(t)}(\tau)\ket{\tau}\,\dd \tau, \qquad
    \bra{\phi_\tau^{(t)}} = \int_{\Rbb} \phi_\tau^{(t)*}(\tau)\bra{\tau}\,\dd \tau,
\end{align}
such that
\begin{align}
    \phi_K^*(k)\psi_K(k) = g(k), \qquad
    \phi_\tau^{(t)*}(\tau)\psi_\tau^{(t)}(\tau) = \mathbbm{1}_{[0,t]}(\tau),
\end{align}
where $\mathbbm{1}_{[0,t]}$ is the indicator function on the interval $[0,t]$.

We further define the joint preparation state and post-selection map
\begin{align}
    \ket{\Psi_{\mathrm{inh}}(t)}
    &:= \ket{\psi_K}\otimes \ket{\psi_\tau^{(t)}} \otimes \ket{b}, \\
    \bra{\Phi_{\mathrm{inh}}(t)}
    &:= \bra{\phi_K}\otimes \bra{\phi_\tau^{(t)}} \otimes I_q .
\end{align}
The corresponding hybrid unitary on $\mathcal{H}_K \otimes \mathcal{H}_{\tau} \otimes \mathcal{H}_q$ is
\begin{align}
    W_{\mathrm{inh}}(t)
    := \exp\!\left[-i\left(\hat{k}\otimes \hat{\tau}\otimes L + I_K \otimes \hat{\tau}\otimes H\right)\right].
\end{align}
Its action is diagonal in the oscillator variables. For any $\ket{\varphi} \in \mathcal{H}_q$,
\begin{align}
    \left(\hat{k}\otimes \hat{\tau}\otimes L + I_K \otimes \hat{\tau}\otimes H\right)\ket{k,\tau}\ket{\varphi}
    = \ket{k,\tau}\,\tau(kL+H)\ket{\varphi},
\end{align}
and therefore
\begin{align}
    W_{\mathrm{inh}}(t)
    = \int_{\Rbb}\int_{\Rbb} \ketbra{k,\tau}{k,\tau} \otimes e^{-i\tau(kL+H)} \, \dd k \, \dd \tau .
\end{align}
Taking the oscillator partial inner product yields
\begin{align}
    \bra{\Phi_{\mathrm{inh}}(t)} W_{\mathrm{inh}}(t)\ket{\Psi_{\mathrm{inh}}(t)} &= \int_{\Rbb}\int_{\Rbb}
    \phi_K^*(k)\psi_K(k)\phi_\tau^{(t)*}(\tau)\psi_\tau^{(t)}(\tau)
    e^{-i\tau(kL+H)}\ket{b}
    \, \dd k \, \dd \tau \nonumber \\
    &\quad = \int_0^t \int_{\Rbb} g(k)e^{-i\tau(kL+H)}\ket{b} \, \dd k \, \dd \tau
    = u_{\mathrm{inh}}(t).
    \label{eq:LCHS-inhomo-cv-realization}
\end{align}
Therefore, the CV-DV realization of the inhomogeneous term is a continuous linear combination of the unitaries $e^{-i\tau(kL+H)}$ over two oscillator variables: the LCHS kernel variable $k$ and the elapsed propagation time $\tau$. Compared with the homogeneous construction, the additional ingredient is the second oscillator that encodes the time-window weight $\mathbbm{1}_{[0,t]}(\tau)$. For a time-dependent source term $b(s)$, the fixed qubit input $\ket{b}$ must be replaced by a source-time entangled state, which introduces an additional source-state preparation oracle in the qubit space.

\subsection{Toy Example: Damped Harmonic Oscillator}


Consider a damped harmonic oscillator with position $x(t)$, damping rate $\gamma \ge 0$, and angular frequency $\omega > 0$ and the underdamped condition $\omega^2 - \gamma^2/4 > 0$. Its equation of motion is
\begin{equation}
\frac{\dd^2 x}{\dd t^2} + \gamma \frac{\dd x}{\dd t} + \omega^2 x = 0 .
\end{equation}
Introducing a new constant $\kappa:=\sqrt{\omega^2 - \gamma^2/4}$ and a new variable $v:= \frac{1}{\kappa} (\dot{x}+\frac{\gamma}{2}x)$, this second-order equation can be written as a first-order linear system
\begin{equation}
\frac{\dd}{\dd t}
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\ v
\end{bmatrix}
=
-
\begin{bmatrix}
\gamma/2 & -\kappa \\
\kappa & \gamma/2
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\ v
\end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:DHO}
\end{equation}
We denote the coefficient matrix by $A$.

To cast the dynamics into the LCHS framework, we perform the Cartesian decomposition of $A$,
\begin{align}
L &= \frac{A + A^\dagger}{2}
   = \frac{\gamma}{2} I \succeq 0, \\
H &= \frac{A - A^\dagger}{2i}
   = - \kappa Y ,
\end{align}
where $I$ is the identity matrix and $Y$ is the Pauli-$Y$ matrix.

The corresponding unitary operators appearing in the LCHS circuit construction are therefore
\begin{align}
    \exp(-i t k L)
    &=
    \exp\!\left(
    - i I \frac{\gamma t k}{2}
    \right), \\
    \exp(-i t H)
    &=
    \exp\!\left(
    i \kappa t Y
    \right).
\end{align}
% Thus, the dissipative contribution is captured by a uniform decay factor, 
% while the Hamiltonian part corresponds to a planar rotation generated by $Y$. The resulting LCHS simulation can be implemented using single-qubit $R_Y$ rotations together with non-unitary damping steps, with standard Trotterization over discrete time steps.
Thus, the dissipative contribution associated with $L = \frac{\gamma}{2} I$ is encoded in each LCHS branch as the $k$-dependent phase $e^{-i(\gamma t k/2)}$, while the Hamiltonian contribution generates the qubit rotation $e^{i \kappa t Y}$. The physical damping factor is recovered only after the CV-weighted integral and post-selection.

\subsection{Example: Heat Equation}
\subsubsection{One-dimensional Dirichlet Case}
\label{1d-heat}

Consider the one-dimensional heat equation
\begin{align}
    \frac{\partial u(x,t)}{\partial t} = \alpha \frac{\partial^2 u(x,t)}{\partial x^2},
    \qquad 0 < x < \mathcal{L},
\end{align}
with initial condition $u(x,0)=f(x)$ and homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions
\begin{align}
    u(0,t)=u(\mathcal{L},t)=0.
\end{align}
Let $M=2^m$ denote the number of interior grid points, let $h=\mathcal{L}/(M+1)$, and write $u_j(t)=u(x_j,t)$ for the interior values. The second-order central difference approximation gives the semidiscrete ODE
\begin{align}
    \frac{\dd}{\dd t}\bm{u}(t) = -A_m^{(\mathrm D)} \bm{u}(t),
    \qquad
    A_m^{(\mathrm D)} := \frac{\alpha}{h^2}T_m^{(\mathrm D)},
\end{align}
where $\bm{u}(t)=(u_1(t),\dots,u_M(t))^\top$ and the Dirichlet Laplacian is
\begin{align}
    T_m^{(\mathrm D)}
    =
    2I^{\otimes m}
    -
    \sum_{x=0}^{M-2}\left(\ketbra{x}{x+1}+\ketbra{x+1}{x}\right).
\end{align}

We order the qubits from least to most significant as $q_0,\dots,q_{m-1}$. For $m=2$,
\begin{align}
    T_2^{(\mathrm D)}
    :=
    \begin{bmatrix}
        2 & -1 & 0 & 0 \\
        -1 & 2 & -1 & 0 \\
        0 & -1 & 2 & -1 \\
        0 & 0 & -1 & 2
    \end{bmatrix}
    =
    2 I_0 I_1 - I_0 X_1 - \frac{1}{2}\left(X_0X_1 + Y_0Y_1\right),
\end{align}
Since $A_m^{(\mathrm D)}$ is real symmetric, its Cartesian decomposition satisfies
\begin{align}
    L = A_m^{(\mathrm D)},
    \qquad
    H = \mathbf{0}.
\end{align}
Therefore one hybrid CV-DV product-formula step takes the form
\begin{align}
    \mathcal U_m^{(\mathrm D)}(\delta t)
    :=
    \exp\!\left(-i\theta\,\hat x \otimes T_m^{(\mathrm D)}\right),
    \qquad
    \theta := \frac{\alpha\,\delta t}{h^2},
\end{align}
where $\delta t=t/n$ for $n$ product-formula steps over total runtime $t$.

The Dirichlet Laplacian admits the decomposition
\begin{align}
    T_m^{(\mathrm D)} = 2I^{\otimes m} - \sum_{c=0}^{m-1} R_c,
    \qquad
    R_c = \sigma_c^+ \prod_{j=0}^{c-1}\sigma_j^- + \sigma_c^- \prod_{j=0}^{c-1}\sigma_j^+,
\end{align}
with
\[
\sigma_j^\pm := \frac{X_j \mp iY_j}{2}.
\]
The first few terms are
\begin{align}
    R_0 = X_0 \text{ and } R_1 = \frac{1}{2}\left(X_1X_0 + Y_1Y_0\right), 
\end{align}
So, $T_2^{(\mathrm D)} = 2 I_0 I_1 -  R_0 - R_1$. Using a first-order product formula, one step is compiled as
\begin{align}
    \mathcal U_m^{(\mathrm D)}(\delta t)
    \approx
    e^{-i2\theta\,\hat x \otimes I^{\otimes m}}
    \prod_{c=0}^{m-1} e^{i\theta\,\hat x \otimes R_c}.
\end{align}
Since $A_m^{(\mathrm D)}$ is real symmetric, the only product-formula error comes from the inner compilation of $T_m^{(\mathrm D)}$ into the blocks $\{2I^{\otimes m}, R_0,\dots,R_{m-1}\}$. The identity block commutes with every $R_c$, so only distinct pairs $R_c$ and $R_{c'}$ contribute.
\begin{equation}
    [\hat{x}\otimes R_c,\;\hat{x}\otimes R_{c'}]=\hat{x}^2\otimes[R_c,R_{c'}],
\end{equation}
Because $\|R_c\|=1$, each commutator obeys $\|[R_c,R_{c'}]\|\le 2$, and there are $\binom{m}{2}=\mathcal{O}(\log^2 M)$ such pairs. Accumulating over $n$ product-formula steps with $\delta t=t/n$ therefore gives
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon_{\mathrm{heat}}=\mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{t^2}{n}\,\frac{\alpha^2}{h^4}\,\|\hat{x}\|^2\,\log^2 M\right).
    \label{eq:heat-trotter-error}
\end{equation}
The step count $n$ is then chosen so that $\epsilon_{\mathrm{heat}}$ stays below the desired tolerance.
Each $R_c$ contains $2^c$ Pauli strings with coefficients $\pm 2^{-c}$,
\[
R_c = 2^{-c}\sum_{\ell=1}^{2^c}s_{c,\ell}P_{c,\ell},
\qquad
s_{c,\ell}\in\{\pm 1\},
\]
where each $P_{c,\ell}$ has support on $q_0,\dots,q_c$ and an even number of $Y$ operators. Hence any two $P_{c,\ell}$ differ on an even number of qubits and commute, so
\begin{align}
    e^{i\theta\,\hat x\otimes R_c}
    =
    \prod_{\ell=1}^{2^c}
    e^{i\theta 2^{-c}s_{c,\ell}\,\hat x\otimes P_{c,\ell}}.
\end{align}
Each factor $e^{i\theta 2^{-c}s_{c,\ell}\,\hat x\otimes P_{c,\ell}}$ is implemented by basis changes, a parity CNOT ladder on the support of $P_{c,\ell}$, one hybrid interaction, and uncomputation. A representative hybrid Pauli block is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:heat_h2_block}.

\subsubsection{Other One-dimensional Boundary Conditions}

Both remaining boundary conditions are obtained from the Dirichlet block by adding correction terms. For periodic boundary conditions,
\begin{align}
    T_m^{(\mathrm P)} = T_m^{(\mathrm D)} - T_m^{\mathrm{corr}},
    \qquad
    T_m^{\mathrm{corr}} := \ketbra{0}{M-1} + \ketbra{M-1}{0}.
\end{align}
The Pauli decomposition of the correction term is
\begin{align}
    T_m^{\mathrm{corr}}
    =
    \ketbra{0^m}{1^m} + \ketbra{1^m}{0^m}
    =
    \prod_{j=0}^{m-1}\sigma_j^+ + \prod_{j=0}^{m-1}\sigma_j^-
    =
    2^{1-m}
    \sum_{\substack{S\subseteq\{0,\dots,m-1\}\\ |S|\ \mathrm{even}}}
    (-1)^{|S|/2} \left(\prod_{j\notin S}X_j\right)\left(\prod_{j\in S}Y_j\right).
\end{align}
Thus $T_m^{\mathrm{corr}}$ contributes $2^{m-1}$ commuting Pauli strings, each of weight $m$.

For homogeneous Neumann boundary conditions,
\begin{align}
    T_m^{(\mathrm N)} = T_m^{(\mathrm D)} - T_m^{\mathrm{corr}},
    \qquad
    T_m^{\mathrm{corr}} := \ketbra{0}{0} + \ketbra{M-1}{M-1}.
\end{align}
Similar to the periodic boundary conditions,
\begin{align}
    T_m^{\mathrm{corr}} =
    \ketbra{0^m}{0^m} + \ketbra{1^m}{1^m} =
    2^{1-m}
    \sum_{\substack{S\subseteq\{0,\dots,m-1\}\\ |S|\ \mathrm{even}}}
    \left(\prod_{j\in S}Z_j\right)\left(\prod_{j\notin S}I_j\right).
\end{align}
Hence $T_m^{\mathrm{corr}}$ contributes $2^{m-1}$ commuting $Z$-type strings, one of which is the identity. In both cases one reuses
\begin{align}
    \mathcal U_m^{(\mathrm{bc})}(\delta t)
    :=
    \exp\!\left(-i\theta\,\hat x \otimes T_m^{(\mathrm{bc})}\right).
\end{align}
The boundary condition changes the Pauli list, but not the hybrid Trotterization pattern.

\subsubsection{$d$-dimensional Extension}

Let $\Omega=\prod_{i=1}^d(0,L_i)$ and consider the $d$-dimensional heat equation
\begin{align}
    \frac{\partial u(\mathbf{x},t)}{\partial t}
    =
    \alpha \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{\partial^2 u(\mathbf{x},t)}{\partial x_i^2},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{x}\in\Omega,
\end{align}
with separable homogeneous boundary conditions $\mathrm{bc}_i\in\{\mathrm D,\mathrm P,\mathrm N\}$ along each axis. Let the $i$th coordinate be discretized with $M_i=2^{m_i}$ grid points and spacing $h_i$, and denote by $I_{m_i}=I^{\otimes m_i}$ the identity on the corresponding coordinate subregister. The semidiscrete operator is the Kronecker sum
\begin{align}
    A_d^{(\mathbf{bc})}
    =
    \alpha \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{1}{h_i^2}
    \Bigl(
        I_{m_1}\otimes \cdots \otimes I_{m_{i-1}}
        \otimes T_{m_i}^{(\mathrm{bc}_i)}
        \otimes I_{m_{i+1}} \otimes \cdots \otimes I_{m_d}
    \Bigr),
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{bc}=(\mathrm{bc}_1,\dots,\mathrm{bc}_d)$. The coordinate blocks commute because they act on disjoint qubit subregisters. Hence
\begin{align}
    \mathcal U_d^{(\mathbf{bc})}(\delta t)
    &:=
    \exp\!\left(-i\delta t\,\hat x \otimes A_d^{(\mathbf{bc})}\right) \nonumber \\
    &=
    \prod_{i=1}^d
    \exp\!\left[
        -i\theta_i\,\hat x \otimes
        \Bigl(
            I_{m_1}\otimes \cdots \otimes I_{m_{i-1}}
            \otimes T_{m_i}^{(\mathrm{bc}_i)}
            \otimes I_{m_{i+1}} \otimes \cdots \otimes I_{m_d}
        \Bigr)
    \right],
\end{align}
with $\theta_i=\alpha\delta t/h_i^2$. Each factor is compiled by the corresponding one-dimensional block.

\subsubsection{Gate Counts \cb{(MZ: match with Section 3, maybe not necessary)}}

For the Dirichlet block, the identity term contributes one unconditional oscillator phase. For each $c$, the operator $R_c$ contributes $2^c$ commuting Pauli factors. After parity mapping, each Pauli factor is implemented by one primitive hybrid gate
\[
e^{-i\lambda \hat x\otimes Z_t},
\]
that is, one conditional displacement on the oscillator, together with $c$ CNOTs to collect parity and $c$ more to uncompute it. Therefore the exact per-step counts are
\begin{align}
    N_{\mathrm{hyb},1}^{(\mathrm D)}(m)
    &=
    1+\sum_{c=0}^{m-1}2^c
    = 2^m = M, \\
    N_{\mathrm{CNOT},1}^{(\mathrm D)}(m)
    &=
    2\sum_{c=0}^{m-1} c\,2^c
    =
    2\bigl[(m-2)2^m + 2\bigr].
\end{align}
If basis changes are counted as well, then each Pauli string of weight $c+1$ uses at most $4(c+1)$ single-qubit Clifford gates, so
\begin{align}
    N_{1q,1}^{(\mathrm D)}(m)
    \le
    4\sum_{c=0}^{m-1}(c+1)2^c
    =
    4\bigl[(m-1)2^m + 1\bigr].
\end{align}
Periodic and Neumann boundary conditions are obtained by adding the costs of $T_m^{\mathrm{corr}}$ or $T_m^{\mathrm{corr}}$ to the Dirichlet counts. For the periodic correction,
\begin{align}
    \Delta N_{\mathrm{hyb},1}^{(\mathrm P)}(m)
    &= 2^{m-1}, \\
    \Delta N_{\mathrm{CNOT},1}^{(\mathrm P)}(m)
    &= 2(m-1)2^{m-1}, \\
    \Delta N_{1q,1}^{(\mathrm P)}(m)
    &\le 4m\,2^{m-1},
\end{align}
since $T_m^{\mathrm{corr}}$ contributes $2^{m-1}$ weight-$m$ strings. For the Neumann correction, the identity term in $T_m^{\mathrm{corr}}$ merges into the unconditional phase, while the remaining strings are already diagonal:
\begin{align}
    \Delta N_{\mathrm{hyb},1}^{(\mathrm N)}(m)
    &= 2^{m-1}-1, \\
    \Delta N_{\mathrm{CNOT},1}^{(\mathrm N)}(m)
    &= 2\sum_{\substack{S\subseteq\{0,\dots,m-1\}\\ |S|\ \mathrm{even},\,S\neq\emptyset}}
    (|S|-1), \\
    \Delta N_{1q,1}^{(\mathrm N)}(m)
    &= 0.
\end{align}
Thus the one-step counts for boundary condition $\mathrm{bc}\in\{\mathrm P,\mathrm N\}$ are obtained by adding the corresponding correction terms to the Dirichlet counts. After $n$ product-formula steps, all three gate counts scale linearly with $n$.

For $d$ dimensions, the coordinate factors commute, so the total counts are sums of the one-dimensional costs:
\begin{align}
    N_{\mathrm{hyb}}^{(d)}
    &= n\sum_{i=1}^d N_{\mathrm{hyb},1}^{(\mathrm{bc}_i)}(m_i), \\
    N_{\mathrm{CNOT}}^{(d)}
    &= n\sum_{i=1}^d N_{\mathrm{CNOT},1}^{(\mathrm{bc}_i)}(m_i), \\
    N_{1q}^{(d)}
    &\le
    n\sum_{i=1}^d N_{1q,1}^{(\mathrm{bc}_i)}(m_i).
\end{align}
When the same boundary condition is imposed on each axis, these expressions reduce to the asymptotic summary in Table~\ref{tab:heat-gate-scaling}. Mixed boundary conditions are obtained by the axis-wise sums above. The only approximation here is the one-dimensional product formula used within each factor. Since the coordinate factors act on disjoint qubit subregisters they commute exactly, the $d$-dimensional split introduces no additional product-formula error. The total error is therefore
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon_{\mathrm{heat}}^{(d)} = \sum_{i=1}^d \epsilon_{\mathrm{heat}}^{(i)},
\end{equation}
where each $\epsilon_{\mathrm{heat}}^{(i)}$ is estimated by Eq.~\eqref{eq:heat-trotter-error} with the grid spacing $h_i$ and Laplacian dimension $m_i$ of axis $i$.

\section{Circuit-Level Realization}

In this section we describe the circuit-level realization used in our numerical study of the time-independent homogeneous CV-DV LCHS map introduced in Section~3.
Writing $A=L+iH$ with $L \succeq 0$, the implemented qubit-space map for runtime $T$ is
\begin{equation}
K(T):=
(\bra{\phi_r}_{\rm osc}\otimes I_q)\,
U_{\mathrm{evol}}(T)\,
(\ket{\psi_{r,r',\beta}}_{\rm osc}\otimes I_q),
\label{eq:impl-map}
\end{equation}
with the hybrid evolution operator
\begin{equation}
U_{\mathrm{evol}}(T):=
e^{-iT(\hat{x}\otimes L+I_{\rm osc}\otimes H)},
\label{eq:impl-uevol}
\end{equation}
the post-selection state (squeezed vacuum)
\begin{equation}
\ket{\phi_r}=S(r)\ket{0},
\label{eq:impl-post}
\end{equation}
and the preparation state (squeezed finite Fock superposition)
\begin{equation}
\ket{\psi_{r,r',\beta}}=S(r')\sum_{n=0}^{N_{\rm max}-1} C_n\ket{n},
\label{eq:impl-prep}
\end{equation}
where $N_{\rm max}$ is the number of retained Fock coefficients.
In practice, $U_{\mathrm{evol}}(T)$ is synthesized by a product-formula approximation as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:trotter}.

For a specific initial vector $\bm{u}(0)$, the post-selected DV output is
$\bm{u}_{\mathrm{cv}}=K(T)\bm{u}(0)$ and the reference is
$\bm{u}_{\mathrm{ref}}=e^{-AT}\bm{u}(0)$.
We report the state fidelity
\begin{equation}
F=\left|\frac{\langle\bm{u}_{\mathrm{cv}},\bm{u}_{\mathrm{ref}}\rangle}
{\|\bm{u}_{\mathrm{cv}}\|\;\|\bm{u}_{\mathrm{ref}}\|}\right|^2,
\label{eq:impl-fidelity}
\end{equation}
and the post-selection probability
\begin{equation}
p_{\mathrm{post}}=\|\bm{u}_{\mathrm{cv}}\|_2^2,
\label{eq:impl-ppost}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{u}_{\mathrm{cv}}$ denotes the unnormalized DV-register amplitudes obtained after projecting
the oscillator mode onto $\bra{\phi_r}$.

\subsection{CV state preparation}
\label{ss:cv-prep}

The truncated Fock coefficients $\{C_n\}_{n=0}^{N_{\rm max}-1}$ are obtained by evaluating the integral in Eq.~\eqref{eq:coefficients} numerically via Gaussian quadrature, using the near-optimal kernel $g_\beta(k)$ defined in Section~2. Under the $\hbar=2$ convention adopted throughout this paper, the Gaussian damping factor in that integral is $\gamma=e^{-2r'}-e^{-2r}$ with squeezing widths $\sigma=e^r$ and $\sigma'=e^{r'}$.
The corresponding oscillator seed state
\[
\ket{\psi_{\mathrm{finite}}}=\sum_{n=0}^{N_{\rm max}-1}C_n\ket{n}
\]
can then be synthesized in two ways.

\paragraph{SNAP-plus-displacement (SNAP+D) synthesis.}
One route uses universal oscillator control generated by number-dependent phase gates together with displacements~\cite{heeres2015cavity, Krastanov2015}. In this picture one seeks a variational approximation of the form
\begin{equation}
U_{\mathrm{SNAP+D}}=\prod_{\ell=1}^{L}D(\alpha_\ell)\,\mathrm{SNAP}(\bm{\theta}_\ell),
\end{equation}
optimized for
\begin{equation}
\max_{\{\alpha_\ell,\bm{\theta}_\ell\}}
\left|\bra{\psi_{\mathrm{finite}}}
U_{\mathrm{SNAP+D}}\ket{0}\right|^2.
\end{equation}
This route is implemented directly at the circuit level in the present numerical framework (Bosonic Qiskit).

\paragraph{Givens decomposition using the Law-Eberly Protocol.}
An alternative route begins from an exact adjacent-level Givens decomposition of the target truncated state. For the two-level rotation acting on adjacent Fock levels $\ket{n}$, $\ket{n+1}$, we define
\begin{equation}
G(n,n{+}1;\theta,\varphi)
=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -e^{-i\varphi}\sin\theta\\
e^{i\varphi}\sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{pmatrix}
\oplus I_{\perp},
\label{eq:givens-rotation}
\end{equation}
where the $2\times2$ block acts on the $\{\ket{n},\ket{n+1}\}$ subspace
and $I_\perp$ is the identity on all other Fock levels.
Any $N_{\rm max}$-component target state can be factored as
\begin{equation}
\ket{\psi_{\mathrm{finite}}}
=G(N_{\rm max}{-}2,N_{\rm max}{-}1;\theta_{N_{\rm max}-1},\varphi_{N_{\rm max}-1})\cdots G(1,2;\theta_2,\varphi_2)
\,G(0,1;\theta_1,\varphi_1)\,\ket{0},
\label{eq:givens-decomposition}
\end{equation}
where the angles $\{(\theta_k,\varphi_k)\}_{k=1}^{N_{\rm max}-1}$ are computed
recursively from the coefficients $C_n$ by a top-down elimination.

In our numerical simulations, the Bosonic Qiskit framework provides only a global Jaynes--Cummings (JC) interaction that couples all Fock manifolds simultaneously with $\sqrt{n+1}$-dependent Rabi frequencies; it cannot implement the number-selective rotations required by the Givens decomposition without severe crosstalk. We therefore adopt \emph{direct state injection}: the Givens angles are computed analytically, the resulting target state $\ket{\psi_{\mathrm{finite}}}$ is constructed classically, and the state vector is loaded directly into the oscillator register as the initial condition for the subsequent hybrid evolution. Because the injection is exact (up to numerical precision), this route and the SNAP+D route differ only in the state-preparation stage; the hybrid propagation and post-selection are identical.

On physical hardware, the Givens rotations map naturally onto the Law--Eberly protocol~\cite{law1996arbitrary, hofheinz2009synthesizing}, which alternates single-qubit rotations with number-selective qubit--oscillator exchange pulses generated by the Jaynes--Cummings interaction
\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{JC}} = \hbar\Omega(t)\bigl(\sigma_- a^\dagger + \sigma_+ a\bigr),
\label{eq:jc-hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
coupling $\ket{e,n}\leftrightarrow\ket{g,n{+}1}$ at rate $\Omega_{n}=\Omega_0\sqrt{n+1}$.
This interaction is available through pulse-level control in superconducting cavity QED platforms~\cite{hofheinz2009synthesizing}. Accordingly, the Givens rotation count ($N_{\rm max}-1$ JC pulses and qubit rotations) should be interpreted as the physical resource cost of a hardware Law--Eberly realization, not as operations executed in the present simulations.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:givens-le} summarizes the overall Givens-based workflow.
\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Givens-based oscillator-state preparation and hybrid LCHS evolution}
\label{alg:givens-le}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Truncated coefficients $\{C_n\}_{n=0}^{N_{\rm max}-1}$, squeezing parameters $(r,r')$, runtime $T$
\Ensure Post-selected DV output $\bm{u}_{\mathrm{cv}}$
\State Normalize the truncated coefficient vector $\{C_n\}$ and compute the Givens angles $\{(\theta_k,\varphi_k)\}_{k=1}^{N_{\rm max}-1}$ by backward elimination so that Eq.~\eqref{eq:givens-decomposition} holds.
\State Construct the oscillator seed state $\ket{\psi_{\mathrm{finite}}}=\sum_{n}C_n\ket{n}$.
\Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{(Simulation: direct state injection.  Hardware: Law--Eberly JC pulse sequence.)}
\State Apply the squeezing operator $S(r')$ to obtain $\ket{\psi_{r,r',\beta}}$.
\State Execute the product-formula circuit for $U_{\mathrm{evol}}(T)$ (Section~\ref{sec:trotter}).
\State Post-select on $\bra{\phi_r}=\bra{0}S^\dagger(r)$ and extract $\bm{u}_{\mathrm{cv}}$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Gate-level compilation of hybrid evolution}
\label{sec:impl-trotter}
Built upon Section~\ref{sec:trotter}, the compiled evolution consists of hybrid factors $e^{-i\Delta t\,\alpha_i\hat{x}\otimes P_i}$ and qubit-only factors $e^{-i\Delta t\,\beta_j Q_j}$. The latter are standard DV Pauli rotations, so the only new CV-DV implementation ingredient is the compilation of a single hybrid factor $e^{-i\Delta t\,c_m\hat{x}\otimes P_m}$. Each Pauli term is compiled by a basis change $B_m$ that diagonalizes $P_m$ into the $Z$ basis,
followed by a parity mapping $C_m$ onto a target qubit $t$:
\begin{equation}
e^{-i\Delta t\,c_m\hat{x}\otimes P_m}
=
B_m^\dagger C_m^\dagger\;
e^{-i\Delta t\,c_m\hat{x}\otimes Z_t}\;
C_m B_m.
\label{eq:impl-parity}
\end{equation}
The primitive hybrid interaction admits the conditional-displacement form
\begin{equation}
e^{-i\lambda \hat{x}\otimes Z_t}
=
\ket{0}\bra{0}_t \otimes D(-i\lambda)
+
\ket{1}\bra{1}_t \otimes D(i\lambda),
\end{equation}
where $D(\alpha)=e^{\alpha a^\dagger-\alpha^* a}$ and, under the $\hbar=2$ convention,
\[
e^{-i\lambda \hat x}=D(-i\lambda).
\]

\section{Conclusion and future works}

We have introduced a hybrid oscillator-qubit formulation of linear-combination-of-Hamiltonian simulation for linear differential equations, with emphasis on the heat equation as a representative benchmark. 
The central idea is to replace the discrete quadrature register of standard DV LCHS by a continuous-variable ancillary mode, so that the LCHS integral is realized through a qumode sandwich acting on a joint oscillator-qubit evolution. This yields a CV-DV LCHS framework in which the qubit register carries the system dynamics, while the oscillator mediates the continuous superposition over LCHS weights. 
Within this setting, we derived the corresponding state-preparation conditions, established truncation- and product-formula error bounds, and gave a gate-level compilation in terms of Gaussian CV operations, controlled displacements, and standard qubit Pauli compilation.

For the one-dimensional heat equation with Dirichlet, Neumann, and periodic boundary conditions, the numerical results show that the ideal-loading CV-DV construction reaches exact solution fidelities above $0.9989$ in all three cases. The empirical trends are consistent with the theoretical analysis: increasing the retained oscillator truncation improves accuracy, and the hybrid product formula exhibits the expected first-order behavior. At the circuit level, the distinction between oracle preparation and hybrid propagation is also clear. Once the oscillator seed is fixed, the hybrid evolution tracks the finite-Fock reference with essentially unit fidelity, so the dominant practical error is due to CV state preparation rather than the subsequent CV-DV dynamics.

The practical state-preparation study further shows that this bottleneck can be substantially mitigated. With optimized SNAP$+$D synthesis, the end-to-end PDE fidelity reaches $0.996817$ for Dirichlet, $0.999484$ for Neumann, and $0.999531$ for periodic boundary conditions. In parallel, the analytic Givens construction matches the ideal-loading baseline to numerical precision and provides a hardware-oriented reference in terms of JC-pulse and qubit-rotation counts. Together, these results indicate that the CV-DV LCHS architecture is not limited by the hybrid evolution principle itself; rather, its near-term performance is governed by how efficiently the required oscillator oracle state can be synthesized.

The comparison with DV LCHS suggests a potentially favorable resource regime for the hybrid approach. On the representation side, the present CV study uses $n_{\mathrm{coeff}}=48$ retained Fock amplitudes, whereas the corresponding DV quadrature constructions for the same heat-equation benchmarks require $M_{\mathrm{DV}}=216$--$248$ LCU terms at $\beta=0.8$. This indicates that the continuous oracle description can be substantially more compact than the corresponding discrete quadrature expansion. On the circuit side, the picture is more nuanced: MPS-compressed DV ancilla preparation can be efficient, but for the Dirichlet benchmark the repeated 100-slice DV circuit remains deeper than the corresponding CV-DV realization. Taken together, these observations suggest that the principal advantage of CV-DV LCHS is not merely a different encoding, but the possibility of reducing the oracle discretization burden while retaining a structured gate decomposition for the system evolution.

Overall, the results support CV-DV LCHS as a viable route for quantum differential equation solving, especially for problems where the LCHS quadrature cost is a dominant limitation of purely discrete implementations. The present study therefore establishes both a theoretical and a circuit-level foundation for hybrid continuous-discrete LCHS, and identifies oscillator-state synthesis as the main remaining ingredient for turning the observed representation advantage into a robust practical resource advantage.
